01 — Food Safety

The basics that keep food safe

Tap any card to open the detail. These are the six things that cause most foodborne illness at home.

🦠

Contamination

How germs, chemicals and foreign objects get into food.

Biological (bacteria, viruses), chemical (cleaning agents, pesticide residue) and physical (hair, glass) hazards. Keep cleaning chemicals far from food prep areas and always cover stored food.

Tap to expand +
🔀

Cross-Contamination

When raw food spreads bacteria to ready-to-eat food.

Use separate boards and knives for raw meat/fish and for vegetables & cooked food. Never place cooked food on a plate that held raw meat. Wash hands between tasks.

Tap to expand +
🌡️

Temperature Danger Zone

5°C to 60°C — where bacteria multiply fastest.

Bacteria double every 20 minutes in this range. Keep hot food above 60°C and cold food below 5°C. Don't leave cooked food at room temperature for more than 2 hours (1 hour in Indian summer heat).

Tap to expand +
📅

Shelf Life

"Best before" vs "Use by" — they are not the same.

Use by is about safety — don't eat after this date. Best before is about quality — food may be safe but less fresh. Learn the full difference in the Myths section.

Tap to expand +
🧊

Food Storage

The fridge order that prevents drips and spoilage.

Top shelves: ready-to-eat & leftovers. Bottom shelf: raw meat & fish (so drips don't fall on other food). Crisper: vegetables. Door: condiments only — it's the warmest zone, not for milk or eggs.

Tap to expand +
🔥

Cooking Temperatures

Heat that actually kills harmful bacteria.

Poultry: 74°C core. Minced meat: 71°C. Fish: 63°C. Reheat leftovers to steaming hot (75°C) all the way through — reheat only once. When in doubt, cook until juices run clear.

04 — Adulteration

Common adulterants & home tests

Simple checks you can do in your own kitchen — based on FSSAI's DART (Detect Adulteration with Rapid Test) methods.

⚠️🥛

Milk

Adulterated with: water, starch, detergent.

Risk: nutrient loss, stomach infection.

Home test: Put a drop on a slanted surface. Pure milk flows slowly leaving a white trail; water-added milk flows fast without a trail. For starch, add iodine — it turns blue.
⚠️🟡

Turmeric

Adulterated with: metanil yellow, chalk.

Risk: metanil yellow is carcinogenic.

Home test: Add a pinch to water — pure turmeric settles and water stays light. Add a few drops of HCl/lemon; a pink-violet colour that stays means synthetic dye.
⚠️🍯

Honey

Adulterated with: sugar syrup, water.

Risk: empty calories, no real benefit.

Home test: Dip a cotton bud in honey and light it. Pure honey burns; adulterated honey with moisture won't catch easily or will crackle.
⚠️🍵

Tea

Adulterated with: used/exhausted tea leaves, colour.

Risk: liver damage from added dyes.

Home test: Rub leaves on wet white paper. Artificial colour leaves a stain on the paper immediately; pure tea does not.
⚠️☕

Coffee

Adulterated with: chicory, tamarind seed powder.

Risk: digestive issues, no caffeine value.

Home test: Sprinkle on water surface. Pure coffee floats; chicory sinks quickly leaving colour streaks behind it.
⚠️🌶️

Chilli Powder

Adulterated with: brick powder, artificial colour.

Risk: heavy metals, stomach damage.

Home test: Sprinkle on water. Brick powder settles as gritty residue you can feel; soap/starch adulterants leave colour streaks that spread.
⚠️🛢️

Mustard Oil

Adulterated with: argemone oil.

Risk: epidemic dropsy, serious illness.

Home test: Add nitric acid to a sample and shake. A reddish-brown colour indicates argemone oil contamination.
⚠️🧈

Ghee

Adulterated with: vanaspati, mashed potato.

Risk: trans fats, heart risk.

Home test: Add a little HCl + sugar to melted ghee and shake. A crimson/red colour in the lower layer indicates vanaspati.
⚠️⚫

Black Pepper

Adulterated with: dried papaya seeds.

Risk: liver toxicity over time.

Home test: Drop into water. Pure pepper sinks; light papaya seeds and hollow, shrivelled fakes float on the surface.
05 — Myths vs Facts

Tap a card to flip the truth

Common food beliefs — and what the science actually says.

Myth

The 5-second rule keeps dropped food safe.

Fact

False.

Bacteria transfer to food instantly on contact. Time barely matters — the surface does.

Myth

Microwaved food becomes radioactive.

Fact

False.

Microwaves only vibrate water molecules to heat food. No radiation stays behind — it's perfectly safe.

Myth

Brown bread is always healthier than white.

Fact

Not always.

Much "brown" bread is white bread with caramel colour. Check for "whole wheat/atta" as the first ingredient.

Myth

"Expiry" and "Best before" mean the same thing.

Fact

No.

Best before = quality guide, often still safe. Expiry/Use-by = safety limit. Don't eat past it.

Myth

Fresh vegetables are always better than frozen.

Fact

Not necessarily.

Frozen veg is often flash-frozen at peak ripeness, locking in nutrients that "fresh" loses over days of transport.

Myth

Washing raw chicken makes it safer.

Fact

Opposite.

Washing splashes bacteria around your sink. Cooking to 74°C is what actually makes it safe.

06 — Kitchen Hygiene

Six steps, start to plate

The WHO "Five Keys" adapted for the Indian kitchen.

🧼Wash Hands

20 seconds with soap before cooking, after handling raw meat, and after using the washroom. Most contamination starts here.

🔪Separate Raw & Cooked

Different boards, knives and containers for raw and ready-to-eat food. Keep raw meat sealed at the bottom of the fridge.

🧽Clean Utensils & Surfaces

Wash boards and counters with hot soapy water. Replace worn sponges and wooden boards with deep grooves.

🔥Cook Properly

Cook to safe core temperatures and reheat leftovers until steaming hot all the way through — only once.

🧊Store Safely

Refrigerate cooked food within 2 hours (1 hour in summer). Cool leftovers quickly; don't overpack the fridge.

💧Use Safe Water & Produce

Wash fruit and vegetables under running water. Use safe water for cooking and for making ice.

09 — Consumer Rights

Found something wrong? Here's what to do

You have the legal right to safe food. Reporting is simpler than most people think.

Keep the evidence

Save the product, the bill, and photos of the batch number, FSSAI licence and expiry date.

Note the FSSAI number

The 14-digit licence number on the pack identifies the manufacturer — it's key to any complaint.

File on the FSSAI Food Safety Connect app or portal

Use the official FSSAI grievance system (foodlicensing / Food Safety Connect) to raise a food safety complaint.

Escalate if unresolved

Approach the Consumer Helpline (1915) or the Consumer Commission under the Consumer Protection Act, 2019.

🔎

Verify before you buy

A genuine FSSAI licence number can be checked on the FSSAI FoSCoS portal. No number, or a fake-looking one, is a red flag.

📢

Know the helplines

FSSAI Food Safety Connect — food safety complaints.
National Consumer Helpline 1915 — consumer grievances.
